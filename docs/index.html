Climate Co-evolution — Write-up

DSC 106 Project 3 · Sungod Warriors · Wanhan Sun, Terry Su, Sohan Shingade · Spring 2026

Motivation

Climate change usually gets reduced to one number: how many degrees warmer. But temperature alone misses half the picture. When the planet warms, precipitation shifts with it. Monsoon belts move, some regions dry out, others flood more. We wanted to ask: how do temperature and precipitation change together across different regions and emissions scenarios between 1950 and 2100?

You can't answer that with a single chart. There are too many combinations of region, scenario, time period, and variable. We needed interaction to let people explore those combinations themselves, rather than trying to pre-select which ones matter.

Dataset

We pulled CMIP6 model output (MPI-ESM1-2-LR) from NOAA's public Google Cloud Storage bucket. We used three emissions scenarios: SSP1-2.6 (strong mitigation, warming stays near 1.5°C), SSP2-4.5 (middle of the road, around 2.5–3°C by 2100), and SSP5-8.5 (high emissions, roughly 4–5°C by 2100).

Data transformations

We subtract the 1981–2010 average to get anomalies relative to that baseline. Monthly data is averaged to annual means. Precipitation comes in kg m⁻² s⁻¹, so we multiply by 86,400 to get mm/day. The chart lines are smoothed with a 10-year centered moving average because the raw yearly data is too noisy to see when scenarios start diverging. Regional averages are weighted by cos(lat) to account for grid cell area. The map is subsampled to about 5° resolution so the page doesn't choke. All of this is noted in the chart subtitles.

Visual encoding

The map uses a 6-bin diverging color scale rather than a continuous one. We found that continuous color made it hard to tell, say, 1.8°C from 2.2°C at a glance. With discrete bins you can immediately see which side of a threshold a cell falls on. The scale is centered at zero so blue means cooling (or wetting) and red means warming (or drying), following IPCC AR6 conventions.

The time series charts encode values on the y-axis, with the three SSP lines separated by color: green for low emissions, yellow for intermediate, red for high.

We thought about using small multiples, one panel per region. But 6 regions times 3 scenarios times 2 variables is 36 panels. Nobody is going to compare panel 4 to panel 29. Linking a map to two charts and letting people switch regions turned out to work much better.

Interaction design

The brush on the temperature chart is the interaction we spent the most time on. You drag a time window, and the map updates to show the average anomaly across that whole range instead of a single decade. This is useful because the spatial pattern of warming actually changes shape over time, and there are too many possible time windows to pre-render.

The decade slider does something simpler: scrub through time and watch the map evolve. A static version of this would need about fifteen side-by-side panels.

Toggling scenarios on and off lets you isolate the counterfactual. Turn off SSP1-2.6 and SSP2-4.5, and you're looking at what happens if we do basically nothing. The map and both charts update together so you see the spatial consequences immediately.

The region selector switches both charts to show just that region's trajectory and highlights it on the map. Clicking through regions is how we found that the Amazon dries under SSP5-8.5 while South Asia gets wetter, even though both are warming. That kind of pattern only shows up when you compare.

Hovering on either chart shows all active SSP values at that year, so you can read the exact gap between scenarios.

Design decisions

Our first version auto-played an animation through the decades. It looked cool but you couldn't stop and stare at a particular period, so we replaced it with a manual slider. We also prototyped a third panel with a temp-vs-precip scatter plot, but three panels felt cramped and the scatter didn't add enough to justify the space. We switched from a continuous to a discrete color scale after reading rubric feedback from previous quarters. The 10-year smoothing stayed because without it the SSP lines overlap until about 2040 and you can't tell them apart.

Development process

Wanhan built the data pipeline (GCS Zarr to JSON), the D3 chart code, and the brush-to-map linking, about 16 hours. Terry did the map rendering, the binned color scale, region outlines, and tooltips, around 12 hours. Sohan handled controls, scenario toggles, user testing, and this writeup, also about 12 hours. Roughly 40 person-hours total.

The data pipeline gave us the most trouble. CMIP6 Zarr stores have non-monotonic time indices and use 0–360° longitude, both of which broke our initial code. The brush-to-map link was also tricky because we have to recompute decade-averaged grids on every brush event. We also kept accidentally growing the chart SVGs on re-render until we figured out we needed to clear them properly.

Two classmates who hadn't seen the project tested it for us. Their feedback is why we added the "drag to select" hint below the temperature chart, the "now" marker at 2024, and clearer subtitle text.

Key findings

The Amazon dries noticeably even under SSP1-2.6, the most optimistic scenario. Polar regions warm fast after 2060 under high emissions. Precipitation responses vary a lot by region in ways that a temperature-only map would never show you. We found most of these patterns by clicking through regions and time windows, which is why the interaction matters.

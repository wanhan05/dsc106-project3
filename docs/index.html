Climate Co-evolution — Write-up

DSC 106 Project 3 · Sungod Warriors · Wanhan Sun, Terry Su, Sohan Shingade · Spring 2026

Motivation

Climate change is usually communicated as a single number — global mean temperature rise. But temperature and precipitation are coupled. Warming shifts monsoon belts, dries some regions, and intensifies rainfall in others. Our central question is: how do temperature and precipitation co-evolve across world regions and emissions scenarios from 1950 to 2100?

This question cannot be answered by a static plot. It requires exploring spatial variation, temporal trajectories, and multivariate relationships across multiple scenarios at once. The interactions are not decorative — they are necessary.

Dataset

We use CMIP6 MPI-ESM1-2-LR model output from the NOAA public Google Cloud Storage bucket, across three Shared Socioeconomic Pathways: SSP1-2.6 (aggressive mitigation, warming stabilizes near 1.5°C), SSP2-4.5 (intermediate, roughly 2.5–3°C by 2100), and SSP5-8.5 (high emissions, roughly 4–5°C by 2100).

Data Transformations

All transformations are stated in the chart subtitles above. We subtract the 1981–2010 climatology to produce anomalies relative to that baseline. Monthly output is averaged to annual means. Precipitation flux in kg m⁻² s⁻¹ is multiplied by 86400 to convert to mm/day. Chart lines show a 10-year centered moving average to reveal long-term trends. Regional aggregates use cos(lat) area weights. The gridded map is subsampled to roughly 5° resolution for performance.

Visual Encoding

The map uses a 6-bin discrete diverging color scale. Discrete bins are easier to read than continuous scales — a viewer can immediately tell whether a cell is above or below 2°C without precise color matching. The diverging structure is centered at zero so warming vs. cooling and wetting vs. drying are obvious from hue direction. We followed IPCC AR6 color conventions.

The time-series charts use position on a quantitative y-axis, the most effective channel for ordered quantitative data. Three SSP lines are separated by hue: green for low emissions, yellow for intermediate, red for high.

We considered small multiples across all regions but 6 regions × 3 scenarios × 2 variables would require 36 panels and make comparison nearly impossible. The linked map and chart design handles this through interaction instead of layout.

Interaction Design

The brush on the temperature chart is the main interaction. Dragging a selection updates the map to show the average anomaly across that entire time window, not just one decade. This reveals how the spatial pattern of warming changes shape across different periods — something impossible to show in any static layout because the number of possible time windows is effectively infinite.

The decade slider lets users scrub through the map to see the spatial pattern evolve over time. A static version would need fifteen panels to replicate this.

Toggling scenarios on and off isolates the counterfactual — what does the world look like under mitigation vs. business-as-usual? The map and both charts update together so users can see the spatial implications of each scenario directly.

The region selector updates both charts and highlights the region on the map. This lets users find that the Amazon dries under SSP5-8.5 while South Asia gets wetter, despite both warming — a multivariate pattern that requires clicking through regions to discover.

Hovering over either chart shows all active SSP values at that year simultaneously, making it easy to read exact divergence between scenarios.

Design Decisions

We tried auto-playing animation first but replaced it with the slider so users have control. We removed a third scatter-plot panel to avoid overloading the layout. We switched from a continuous color scale to 6 discrete bins after reviewing rubric feedback. We kept the 10-year smoothing on the charts because raw annual data obscured scenario divergence; the smoothing is noted in every subtitle.

Development Process

Wanhan worked on the data preprocessing pipeline from GCS to JSON, the D3 chart architecture, the brush-to-map linking, and performance (~16 hrs). Terry worked on the map, binned color scale, region outlines, and tooltips (~12 hrs). Sohan worked on controls, scenario toggles, the writeup, and user testing (~12 hrs). Total around 40 person-hours.

The hardest parts were the data pipeline (CMIP6 Zarr stores have non-monotonic time indices and 0–360° longitude that required careful handling), the brush-to-map linking (decade-averaged grids need recomputing on every brush event), and preventing the chart SVGs from growing on each re-render.

We tested with two classmates who had not seen the visualization. Their feedback led to the brush hint, the "now" marker on 2024, and clearer subtitle text.

Key Findings

The Amazon dries substantially even under SSP1-2.6. Polar warming accelerates sharply after 2060 under high emissions. Precipitation responses are regionally heterogeneous in ways temperature maps alone do not show. These patterns emerge through interactive exploration and would not be visible in any single static plot.
